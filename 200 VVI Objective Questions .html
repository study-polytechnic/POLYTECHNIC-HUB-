SBTE Electrical Circuit & Network - 200 MCQs

1. DC Network Fundamentals

1. What is the fundamental unit of electrical current?
A) VoltB) AmpereC) CoulombD) Ohm
Ans: B) Ampere
The fundamental unit of electrical current is the Ampere. Volt is the unit of voltage, Coulomb is the unit of charge, and Ohm is the unit of resistance.
2. Ohm's law is applicable to:
A) SemiconductorsB) Vacuum tubesC) ElectrolytesD) Linear resistors
Ans: D) Linear resistors
Ohm's law is only applicable to linear and bilateral networks. Semiconductors and vacuum tubes are non-linear devices, so Ohm's law does not apply to them.
3. Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL) is based on the conservation of:
A) EnergyB) MomentumC) ChargeD) Mass
Ans: C) Charge
KCL states that the total current entering a node is equal to the total current leaving it. This is a direct consequence of the conservation of electric charge.
4. Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL) is based on the conservation of:
A) ChargeB) EnergyC) PowerD) Flux
Ans: B) Energy
KVL states that the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop is zero, which is based on the law of conservation of energy.
5. The unit of electrical power is:
A) JouleB) WattC) Volt-Ampere ReactiveD) Coulomb/sec
Ans: B) Watt
Power = Work Done / Time = Joules / second, which is defined as Watt. Joule is the unit of energy, and VAR is the unit of reactive power.
6. Conductance is the reciprocal of:
A) CapacitanceB) InductanceC) ResistanceD) Impedance
Ans: C) Resistance
Conductance (G) represents the ease with which current flows and is the mathematical reciprocal of resistance (R). Thus, G = 1/R.
7. The unit of conductance is:
A) OhmB) Siemens (Mho)C) FaradD) Henry
Ans: B) Siemens (Mho)
Since the unit of resistance is Ohm, its reciprocal, conductance, has the unit Siemens or Mho. Farad is the unit of capacitance, and Henry is the unit of inductance.
8. If two resistors of 10Ω and 10Ω are connected in parallel, the equivalent resistance is:
A) 20ΩB) 5ΩC) 100ΩD) 10Ω
Ans: B) 5Ω
Parallel combination formula: Req = (R1 * R2) / (R1 + R2) = (10 * 10) / (10 + 10) = 100 / 20 = 5Ω. When two identical resistors are in parallel, the equivalent value is halved!
9. If three resistors of 5Ω are in series, the total resistance is:
A) 1.67ΩB) 5ΩC) 15ΩD) 10Ω
Ans: C) 15Ω
In a series combination, the resistances are added directly: Req = R1 + R2 + R3 = 5 + 5 + 5 = 15Ω.
10. Ideal voltage source has an internal resistance of:
A) InfinityB) ZeroC) 1 OhmD) 100 Ohms
Ans: B) Zero
An ideal voltage source maintains a constant output voltage regardless of the current drawn from it. Therefore, its internal series resistance is assumed to be exactly Zero (0).
11. Ideal current source has an internal resistance of:
A) ZeroB) InfinityC) NegativeD) 1 Ohm
Ans: B) Infinity
To supply a constant current regardless of the voltage across its terminals, an ideal current source must have an infinite internal parallel resistance so that no current is diverted internally.
12. Identify the circuit arrangement shown below:
A) Parallel ResistorsB) Series ResistorsC) Capacitors in SeriesD) Inductors in Parallel
Ans: B) Series Resistors
The diagram shows two resistors connected end-to-end along a single continuous path. This represents a classic series connection.
13. In a parallel circuit, which quantity remains the same across all branches?
A) CurrentB) PowerC) VoltageD) Resistance
Ans: C) Voltage
Parallel branches are connected directly across the same two common nodes, which means the voltage drop across every single branch remains identical.
14. In a series circuit, which quantity remains the same across all elements?
A) VoltageB) CurrentC) PowerD) Energy
Ans: B) Current
In a series circuit, there is only one continuous path for the flow of charge. Therefore, the same electric current flows through all connected components.
15. The formula for electrical energy is:
A) P/tB) V*IC) V*I*tD) I^2/R
Ans: C) V*I*t
Electrical Energy is defined as the product of Power and Time. Since Power = V * I, the formula for energy becomes V * I * t.
16. 1 kWh (Kilo-Watt-Hour) is equal to:
A) 3.6 x 10^5 JoulesB) 3.6 x 10^6 JoulesC) 3600 JoulesD) 1000 Joules
Ans: B) 3.6 x 10^6 Joules
1 kWh = 1000 Watts * 3600 seconds = 3.6 * 10^6 Watt-seconds (Joules). This represents one standard 'unit' of electricity billed in domestic households.
17. Temperature coefficient of resistance of a pure metal is generally:
A) NegativeB) ZeroC) PositiveD) Infinite
Ans: C) Positive
Pure metals have a Positive Temperature Coefficient (PTC). As temperature increases, the thermal vibrations of metal atoms increase, which obstructs the flow of electrons, thereby increasing the resistance.
18. Which material has a negative temperature coefficient?
A) CopperB) AluminumC) SemiconductorD) Silver
Ans: C) Semiconductor
Semiconductors and insulators have a Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC). As temperature rises, more electrons gain enough energy to break free into the conduction band, reducing the overall resistance.
19. A node in an electrical network is defined as:
A) A closed pathB) A junction of two or more elementsC) A single componentD) A voltage source
Ans: B) A junction of two or more elements
A node is a junction point in an electrical circuit where two or more circuit elements (like resistors, sources, etc.) connect.
20. A loop in a circuit is:
A) Any closed pathB) A junction pointC) An open circuitD) A grounding point
Ans: A) Any closed path
A loop refers to any closed path in an electrical network that starts and ends at the exact same node, passing through other nodes only once.
21. The algebraic sum of voltages around any closed loop is zero. This is:
A) KCLB) Ohm's LawC) KVLD) Faraday's Law
Ans: C) KVL
This is the direct statement of Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL), which represents the principle of conservation of energy in electrical circuits.
22. The equivalent resistance of a Star network (Ra, Rb, Rc) converted to Delta (Rab, Rbc, Rca) involves:
A) Addition onlyB) Sum of pairwise products divided by opposite resistorC) Direct multiplicationD) Subtraction
Ans: B) Sum of pairwise products divided by opposite resistor
Star-to-Delta Conversion Formula: Rab = Ra + Rb + (Ra*Rb / Rc) = (Ra*Rb + Rb*Rc + Rc*Ra) / Rc. This involves the sum of pairwise products of star resistances divided by the opposite star branch resistor.
23. If three identical resistors R are connected in Delta, the equivalent Star resistance is:
A) 3RB) R/3C) RD) R/2
Ans: B) R/3
When converting a symmetrical Delta network of identical resistors (R) to Star, the equivalent star resistance for each branch is exactly divided by 3, making it R/3.
24. If three identical resistors R are connected in Star, the equivalent Delta resistance is:
A) 3RB) R/3C) RD) 1.5R
Ans: A) 3R
When converting a symmetrical Star network of identical resistors (R) to Delta, the equivalent delta resistance becomes exactly three times the star branch value (3R).
25. Active elements in a circuit are those which:
A) Dissipate energyB) Store energyC) Supply energyD) Both A and B
Ans: C) Supply energy
Active elements (such as generators, batteries, transistors, and op-amps) have the physical capacity to continuously deliver electrical energy to the circuit.
26. Which of the following is a passive element?
A) Voltage SourceB) Current SourceC) ResistorD) Battery
Ans: C) Resistor
A resistor cannot generate or supply electrical energy. It can only absorb and dissipate it in the form of heat, making it a passive element.
27. Specific resistance (Resistivity) depends on:
A) Length of wireB) Area of cross-sectionC) Material nature and temperatureD) Applied voltage
Ans: C) Material nature and temperature
While resistance depends on geometry (length and cross-sectional area), specific resistance (resistivity, ρ) is an intrinsic property that depends solely on the nature of the material and its temperature.
28. The unit of resistivity is:
A) OhmB) Ohm-meterC) Mho-meterD) Ohm/meter
Ans: B) Ohm-meter
From the formula R = ρ * (L/A), we can isolate ρ = R * (A/L). Substituting the standard units gives: Ohm * m^2 / m = Ohm-meter (Ω-m).
29. Power dissipated in a resistor is given by:
A) V/RB) I^2 * RC) I * R^2D) V^2 * I
Ans: B) I^2 * R
The power dissipated in a resistor is governed by Joule's law of heating: P = I^2 * R. Alternative mathematical forms are P = V^2 / R and P = V * I.
30. A short circuit has a resistance of:
A) InfinityB) ZeroC) 100 OhmsD) Negative
Ans: B) Zero
A short circuit represents a direct connection via an ideal conductor. An ideal conducting path has absolutely Zero (0) electrical resistance.
31. An open circuit has a current of:
A) InfinityB) ZeroC) Maximum possibleD) Nominal
Ans: B) Zero
An open circuit represents a completely broken path with infinite resistance. Since the path is physically disconnected, no current can flow (Current = 0).
32. Electrical conductance (G) is related to resistance (R) by:
A) G = RB) G = 1/RC) G = R^2D) G = 1/R^2
Ans: B) G = 1/R
Conductance is defined as the mathematical reciprocal of resistance: G = 1/R.
33. The flow of electric current in metallic conductors is due to:
A) Positive ionsB) ElectronsC) ProtonsD) Neutrons
Ans: B) Electrons
In metals, atoms share their valence electrons in a metallic lattice, creating a "sea of free electrons." When a voltage is applied, these free electrons drift towards the positive potential, constituting an electric current.
34. Which formula represents the current division rule for two parallel resistors R1 and R2?
A) I1 = I * (R1 / (R1+R2))B) I1 = I * (R2 / (R1+R2))C) I1 = V / R2D) I1 = I * R1 * R2
Ans: B) I1 = I * (R2 / (R1+R2))
The Current Division Rule states that the current flowing through any branch is equal to the total current multiplied by the opposite branch resistance, divided by the sum of both resistances: I1 = I * (R2 / (R1 + R2)).
35. Voltage division rule applies primarily to:
A) Parallel circuitsB) Series circuitsC) Both equallyD) Magnetic circuits
Ans: B) Series circuits
In a series circuit, current remains identical but voltage is divided across the series components. Thus, the voltage division rule applies specifically to series configurations.
36. A 10V battery is connected across a 5Ω resistor. The power is:
A) 50 WB) 2 WC) 20 WD) 5 W
Ans: C) 20 W
Using the power formula: P = V^2 / R = 10^2 / 5 = 100 / 5 = 20 Watts (W).
37. What happens to total resistance when more resistors are added in parallel?
A) IncreasesB) DecreasesC) Remains sameD) Becomes zero
Ans: B) Decreases
Adding more branches in parallel provides alternative paths for the current to flow. This reduces the overall opposition to the current, causing the equivalent resistance to decrease.
38. Independent sources are denoted by:
A) Diamond shapeB) Circle shapeC) Square shapeD) Triangle shape
Ans: B) Circle shape
In standard circuit schematic conventions, independent voltage and current sources are represented using a circular symbol.
39. Dependent sources are usually denoted by:
A) Diamond shapeB) Circle shapeC) Rectangle shapeD) Oval shape
Ans: A) Diamond shape
Dependent (or controlled) sources, whose output value depends on another voltage or current elsewhere in the circuit, are represented by a diamond-shaped symbol.
40. Superconductivity is a condition of:
A) Infinite resistanceB) Zero resistanceC) High voltageD) Maximum power
Ans: B) Zero resistance
Superconductivity is a quantum mechanical state where certain materials exhibit exactly Zero (0) electrical resistance when cooled below their characteristic critical transition temperature.

2. Network Theorems

41. Superposition theorem is valid for:
A) Linear circuitsB) Non-linear circuitsC) Both A and BD) Neither A nor B
Ans: A) Linear circuits
The Superposition Theorem is mathematically rooted in the principles of linearity (additivity and homogeneity). Therefore, it can only be applied to linear active networks.
42. Superposition theorem is NOT applicable to calculate:
A) VoltageB) CurrentC) PowerD) All of the above
Ans: C) Power
Power is a non-linear parameter because it depends on the square of the current or voltage (P = I^2*R or V^2/R). Thus, individual power contributions from each source cannot be directly summed using superposition.
43. In Superposition theorem, while considering one source, ideal voltage sources are:
A) Open circuitedB) Short circuitedC) Kept as isD) Replaced by current sources
Ans: B) Short circuited
To deactivate an independent ideal voltage source while keeping its internal resistance intact, we replace it with a Short Circuit (since its internal resistance is zero).
44. In Superposition theorem, ideal current sources are:
A) Open circuitedB) Short circuitedC) DoubledD) Halved
Ans: A) Open circuited
To deactivate an independent ideal current source, we replace it with an Open Circuit (since its internal parallel resistance is infinite).
45. Thevenin’s equivalent circuit consists of:
A) Vth in parallel with RthB) Vth in series with RthC) Ith in series with RthD) Ith in parallel with Rth
Ans: B) Vth in series with Rth
Thevenin's Theorem states that any complex active linear network, when viewed from two terminals, can be replaced by an equivalent circuit containing a single independent voltage source (Vth) connected in series with an equivalent resistance (Rth).
46. Norton’s equivalent circuit consists of:
A) V in series with RB) I in series with RC) I in parallel with RD) V in parallel with R
Ans: C) I in parallel with R
Norton's Theorem is the mathematical dual of Thevenin's Theorem. It reduces a complex network into an equivalent current source (IN) in parallel with an equivalent resistance (RN).
47. Thevenin resistance (Rth) is found by:
A) Shorting load & sourcesB) Looking into terminals with sources killedC) Opening all resistorsD) Shorting the terminals directly
Ans: B) Looking into terminals with sources killed
To find Rth, the load resistor is disconnected (open-circuited). All independent voltage sources are replaced by short circuits and independent current sources by open circuits, and the equivalent input resistance is determined.
48. Maximum power is transferred to a load when load resistance RL is:
A) RL > RthB) RL < RthC) RL = RthD) RL = 0
Ans: C) RL = Rth
According to the Maximum Power Transfer Theorem, maximum power is transferred from a source to a load when the load resistance (RL) is exactly equal to the source's internal equivalent resistance (Rth).
49. The efficiency of maximum power transfer in DC circuits is:
A) 100%B) 75%C) 50%D) 25%
Ans: C) 50%
When RL = Rth, the total power is divided equally between the source resistance and the load resistance. Therefore, the power transfer efficiency is exactly η = PLoad / PTotal = 50%.
50. Norton current (IN) is essentially the:
A) Open circuit voltageB) Short circuit currentC) Average currentD) Leakage current
Ans: B) Short circuit current
The Norton current (IN) is evaluated by short-circuiting the output/load terminals and measuring the current that flows through the shorted branch.
51. Millman’s theorem yields an equivalent:
A) Voltage or Current sourceB) Resistance onlyC) Power sourceD) Inductor
Ans: A) Voltage or Current source
Millman's theorem is a convenient shortcut used to simplify several parallel branches containing voltage sources and resistors into a single equivalent voltage or current source.
52. Reciprocity theorem is applicable to networks that are:
A) Linear & BilateralB) Non-linear & UnilateralC) Active & BilateralD) Time-variant
Ans: A) Linear & Bilateral
The Reciprocity Theorem applies specifically to linear, bilateral, and passive network elements (like resistors, inductors, and capacitors) where the excitation and response can be swapped.
53. Reciprocity theorem states that the ratio of excitation to response is:
A) ZeroB) ConstantC) InfiniteD) Negative
Ans: B) Constant
The Reciprocity Theorem states that if you swap the locations of the voltage source (excitation) and the current measurement (response) in a linear bilateral network, the transfer ratio remains Constant.
54. Tellegen’s theorem is based on the principle of conservation of:
A) ChargeB) VoltageC) PowerD) Current
Ans: C) Power
Tellegen's theorem states that the sum of power delivered or absorbed by all components in an electrical network is exactly zero: Σ (v_k * i_k) = 0. This is based on the conservation of power/energy.
55. Substitution theorem is applicable to:
A) Linear circuits onlyB) Non-linear circuits onlyC) Both linear and non-linearD) AC circuits only
Ans: C) Both linear and non-linear
The Substitution Theorem states that any branch in an active network can be replaced by an equivalent voltage or current source, provided the terminal voltage and current of that branch remain unchanged. It is applicable to both linear and non-linear circuits.
56. Compensation theorem is useful in finding the effect of:
A) Adding a new sourceB) Change in branch resistanceC) Temperature dropD) Frequency change
Ans: B) Change in branch resistance
The Compensation Theorem is used to evaluate the impact on all other branch currents and node voltages when a specific branch resistance undergoes a change of ΔR.
57. For a two-terminal network, Vth = 10V and I_short_circuit = 2A. What is Rth?
A) 20 ΩB) 10 ΩC) 5 ΩD) 0.2 Ω
Ans: C) 5 Ω
The relationship formula is: Rth = Vth / Isc (where Isc is the short-circuit Norton current). Here, Rth = 10V / 2A = 5Ω.
58. Dual of Thevenin’s Theorem is:
A) Superposition TheoremB) Norton’s TheoremC) Millman’s TheoremD) Tellegen’s Theorem
Ans: B) Norton’s Theorem
The dual equivalent of a voltage source in series with a resistor (Thevenin's Model) is a current source in parallel with a resistor (Norton's Model).
59. A linear network contains only:
A) Independent sourcesB) Linear elementsC) Both A and BD) Non-linear elements
Ans: C) Both A and B (linear elements and sources)
A linear network comprises linear, bilateral passive components (R, L, C) as well as active independent energy sources that satisfy the superposition principle.
60. Thevenin’s theorem reduces a complex circuit to:
A) Single RB) Single V source and series RC) Single I sourceD) Many loops
Ans: B) Single V source and series R
The fundamental principle of Thevenin's theorem is to simplify any complex multi-loop circuit down to a single independent voltage source connected in series with a single resistor.
61. While finding Rth, dependent sources are:
A) ShortedB) OpenedC) Left intactD) Replaced by R
Ans: C) Left intact
Because the output of a dependent source is controlled by another circuit variable, they cannot be turned off. They must be left intact and are typically solved using a test voltage/current method.
62. If Vth = 20V and RL = Rth = 10Ω, max power delivered is:
A) 10WB) 20WC) 40WD) 100W
Ans: A) 10W
Maximum Power Formula: Pmax = Vth^2 / (4 * Rth) = (20)^2 / (4 * 10) = 400 / 40 = 10 Watts (W).
63. In Reciprocity theorem, we interchange:
A) Resistors and InductorsB) Source and ResponseC) Voltage and CurrentD) Nodes and Meshes
Ans: B) Source and Response
To verify the Reciprocity Theorem, we swap the physical locations of the excitation source and the resulting measured response. The ratio of excitation to response remains unchanged.
64. Norton's equivalent resistance (Rn) is equal to:
A) RthB) 1/RthC) Rth^2D) 0
Ans: A) Rth
Norton's equivalent resistance (RN) is identical to Thevenin's equivalent resistance (Rth). The process of looking into the terminals with sources deactivated is identical.
65. Source transformation converts a voltage source V in series with R into:
A) I = V*R in parallelB) I = V/R in parallelC) I = R/V in seriesD) I = V/R in series
Ans: B) I = V/R in parallel
Using Ohm's Law and source equivalence, a voltage source V in series with R is mathematically equivalent to a current source I = V/R connected in parallel with the same resistor R.
66. Star-Delta transformation is an application of:
A) SuperpositionB) Network simplificationC) TheveninD) Tellegen
Ans: B) Network simplification
Star-Delta transformations are mathematical tools used for network simplification, especially when resistors are configured in bridges or loops that are neither series nor parallel.
67. If a network has no dependent sources, Rth can be found by:
A) Applying a test voltageB) Deactivating independent sources and simplifyingC) Both A and BD) Neither A nor B
Ans: C) Both A and B
If there are no dependent sources, we can either: (A) apply an external test source Vdc and find Idc, or (B) deactivate independent sources (short voltage, open current) and calculate the equivalent series-parallel resistance. Both methods are correct.
68. Maximum power transfer theorem is used in:
A) Power systemsB) Communication circuits (Impedance matching)C) Home wiringD) Transformers
Ans: B) Communication circuits (Impedance matching)
Power grids prioritize maximum efficiency (>95%). In contrast, communication circuits process small signals where maximizing total power transfer (impedance matching) is the primary goal to avoid signal distortion.
69. In a linear circuit, doubling the source voltage causes the currents to:
A) HalveB) Remain sameC) DoubleD) Quadruple
Ans: C) Double
According to the homogeneity (scaling) property of linear circuits, multiplying the input excitation by a factor of 'k' (doubling) scales the output response (currents) by the exact same factor 'k'.
70. A circuit which satisfies both superposition and homogeneity principles is called:
A) Non-linearB) BilateralC) LinearD) Active
Ans: C) Linear
By mathematical definition, any system or circuit is classified as linear if and only if it strictly satisfies both the principles of Superposition (additivity) and Homogeneity (scaling).
71. The sum of powers absorbed by all elements in a circuit is zero. This is:
A) KVLB) Tellegen’s theoremC) Millman's theoremD) Superposition
Ans: B) Tellegen’s theorem
This is Tellegen's Theorem, which mathematically states that the total sum of power generated by active sources is exactly equal to the total power absorbed by passive components, resulting in a net sum of zero.
72. Reciprocity theorem is NOT applicable for circuits containing:
A) Dependent sourcesB) ResistorsC) InductorsD) Capacitors
Ans: A) Dependent sources
The Reciprocity Theorem requires the circuit elements to be strictly passive and bilateral. Dependent sources are active and non-bilateral, violating this prerequisite.
73. According to Millman’s theorem, equivalent voltage V_eq for parallel branches is:
A) Σ(V/R) / Σ(1/R)B) ΣV / ΣRC) Σ(V*R)D) ΣR / ΣV
Ans: A) Σ(V/R) / Σ(1/R)
Millman's formula computes equivalent voltage as: Veq = (V1*G1 + V2*G2...) / (G1 + G2...), where conductance G = 1/R. This can be expressed as: Σ(V/R) / Σ(1/R).
74. When finding Thevenin's voltage across an open circuit, the current through the open circuit is:
A) MaximumB) InfiniteC) ZeroD) Vth/Rth
Ans: C) Zero
Because an open circuit possesses infinite electrical resistance, the current flowing through those terminals is strictly Zero (0).
75. Can Thevenin's theorem be applied to AC circuits?
A) NoB) Yes, using impedances (Z)C) Only for resistors in ACD) Only at resonance
Ans: B) Yes, using impedances (Z)
Thevenin's Theorem is equally applicable to AC circuits. The only difference is that DC resistances are replaced by complex AC Impedances (Z = R + jX) and voltages are treated as phasors.
76. A circuit is said to be bilateral if its behavior is independent of:
A) Voltage magnitudeB) Current magnitudeC) Direction of currentD) Time
Ans: C) Direction of current
An element is bilateral if it conducts electrical current equally well in both directions, meaning its impedance parameters remain identical regardless of the direction of the current.
77. Which diode circuit violates the Reciprocity Theorem?
A) Unilateral nature of diodeB) High resistanceC) Low capacitanceD) Inductance
Ans: A) Unilateral nature of diode
A diode is a unilateral device (it conducts in forward bias but blocks current in reverse bias). This asymmetry violates the bilateral requirement needed to satisfy the Reciprocity Theorem.
78. A voltage source in series with an open switch has a Thevenin equivalent of:
A) Vth=V, Rth=0B) Vth=0, Rth=InfinityC) Vth=V, Rth=InfinityD) Vth=0, Rth=0
Ans: B) Vth=0, Rth=Infinity
An open switch blocks any current flow and acts as an infinite resistance (Rth = Infinity). Since no energy is transferred across the terminals, the open-circuit voltage is zero (Vth = 0).
79. An ideal current source has zero:
A) Internal resistanceB) Internal conductanceC) VoltageD) Current
Ans: B) Internal conductance
An ideal current source has an infinite internal parallel resistance (R_int = ∞). Since internal conductance (G) is the reciprocal of resistance, G = 1 / R_int = 1 / ∞ = Zero (0).
80. Superposition theorem is widely used for solving networks with:
A) Single sourceB) Multiple sourcesC) No sourcesD) Only dependent sources
Ans: B) Multiple sources
The Superposition Theorem is specifically designed to analyze multi-source networks by calculating the individual contribution of each independent source acting alone.

3. AC Fundamentals

81. The equation of an alternating voltage is v = Vm sin(ωt). Here Vm is:
A) RMS valueB) Average valueC) Peak (Maximum) valueD) Instantaneous value
Ans: C) Peak (Maximum) value
In the sinusoidal wave equation v = Vm sin(ωt), 'Vm' represents the maximum displacement from the zero reference line, commonly known as the Peak (Maximum) Value.
82. RMS value of a sinusoidal wave is related to its peak value (Vm) by:
A) Vrms = Vm / √2B) Vrms = Vm * √2C) Vrms = Vm / 2D) Vrms = Vm / π
Ans: A) Vrms = Vm / √2
The RMS (Root Mean Square) value corresponds to the equivalent heating effect of DC. For a pure sine wave, mathematical integration yields: Vrms = Vm / √2 ≈ 0.707 * Vm.
83. Average value of a full sinusoidal wave over one complete cycle is:
A) Vm/2B) 2Vm/πC) ZeroD) Vm
Ans: C) Zero
Because a pure sine wave is perfectly symmetrical, its positive half-cycle and negative half-cycle are identical. Their sum over one complete cycle cancels out, resulting in an average value of exactly Zero (0).
84. Average value of a half-wave rectified sine wave is:
A) Vm/πB) 2Vm/πC) Vm/2D) Vm/√2
Ans: A) Vm/π
A half-wave rectifier blocks the negative half-cycle and only allows the positive half-cycle to pass. Integrating over the full cycle (2π) yields: Vavg = Vm / π.
85. Form Factor is the ratio of:
A) Peak to RMSB) RMS to AverageC) Average to RMSD) Peak to Average
Ans: B) RMS to Average
Form Factor indicates the shape of an AC wave. It is defined as the mathematical ratio of the RMS value to the Average value: Form Factor = RMS Value / Average Value.
86. Form factor of a pure sinusoidal wave is:
A) 1.414B) 1.11C) 0.707D) 0.637
Ans: B) 1.11
For a pure sine wave, Vrms = 0.707 * Vm and Vavg = 0.637 * Vm. Their ratio (Form Factor) is: 0.707 / 0.637 ≈ 1.11.
87. Peak Factor (Crest Factor) is the ratio of:
A) RMS to AverageB) Average to PeakC) Peak to RMSD) Peak to Average
Ans: C) Peak to RMS
Peak Factor measures how sharp the peak of an AC wave is. It is defined as the ratio of the Peak (Maximum) value to the RMS value: Peak Factor = Maximum Value / RMS Value.
88. Peak factor of a pure sinusoidal wave is:
A) 1.11B) 1.414 (√2)C) 2.0D) 0.707
Ans: B) 1.414 (√2)
For a pure sine wave: Peak Factor = Vm / Vrms = Vm / (Vm / √2) = √2 ≈ 1.414.
89. The angular frequency ω is equal to:
A) 2πfB) πfC) f/2πD) 2π/f
Ans: A) 2πf
Angular frequency (ω), measured in radians per second, is mathematically related to cyclic frequency (f), measured in Hertz, by the relationship: ω = 2πf.
90. In a pure resistive AC circuit, voltage and current are:
A) Out of phase by 90°B) In phaseC) Current leads by 90°D) Voltage leads by 90°
Ans: B) In phase
In a purely resistive circuit, current is directly proportional to voltage at every instant (i = v/R). Both waveforms reach their maximum and zero points at the exact same times, meaning they are perfectly 'In Phase' (Phase angle difference = 0°).
91. In a pure inductive circuit, current:
A) Leads voltage by 90°B) Lags voltage by 90°C) Is in phase with voltageD) Lags voltage by 45°
Ans: B) Lags voltage by 90°
An inductor opposes changes in current by inducing a counter-electromotive force (back-EMF). This causes the current waveform to lag behind the voltage waveform by exactly 90° (π/2 radians).
92. In a pure capacitive circuit, current:
A) Lags voltage by 90°B) Is in phaseC) Leads voltage by 90°D) Leads voltage by 180°
Ans: C) Leads voltage by 90°
In a capacitive circuit, charge must accumulate on the plates before a voltage can develop. Current must flow first, which means current leads the voltage by exactly 90° (π/2 radians).
93. Inductive reactance (XL) is given by:
A) 1 / (2πfL)B) 2πfLC) f / (2πL)D) 2πf/L
Ans: B) 2πfL
Inductive reactance (XL) is the opposition offered by an inductor to the flow of AC. It is directly proportional to the frequency and is calculated as: XL = ω*L = 2πfL.
94. Capacitive reactance (XC) is given by:
A) 2πfCB) 1 / (2πfC)C) 2π/fCD) f / 2πC
Ans: B) 1 / (2πfC)
Capacitive reactance (XC) is the opposition offered by a capacitor to the flow of AC. It is inversely proportional to frequency and is calculated as: XC = 1 / (ω*C) = 1 / (2πfC).
95. Impedance (Z) of a series RL circuit is:
A) R + XLB) √(R^2 + XL^2)C) R - XLD) √(R^2 - XL^2)
Ans: B) √(R^2 + XL^2)
In a series RL circuit, resistance (R) and inductive reactance (XL) are perpendicular to each other in the phasor domain (at 90°). Thus, the total impedance magnitude is calculated as: Z = √(R^2 + XL^2).
96. Power factor (cos φ) of an AC circuit is the ratio of:
A) Apparent Power / Real PowerB) Real Power / Apparent PowerC) Reactive Power / Real PowerD) Z / R
Ans: B) Real Power / Apparent Power
The Power Factor (cos φ) represents the ratio of useful active power (Real Power, in Watts) to total drawn power (Apparent Power, in Volt-Amperes). It can also be expressed as R / Z.
97. The unit of Apparent Power is:
A) Watts (W)B) Volt-Amperes Reactive (VAR)C) Volt-Amperes (VA)D) Joules
Ans: C) Volt-Amperes (VA)
Apparent Power (S = V * I) is the combined phasor sum of real and reactive power. Its unit is Volt-Amperes (VA). Watts is for real power, and VAR is for reactive power.
98. The unit of Reactive Power is:
A) WB) VAC) VARD) V
Ans: C) VAR
Reactive power (Q = V * I * sin φ) is the power that oscillates back and forth between inductive/capacitive elements and the source. It is measured in Volt-Amperes Reactive (VAR).
99. At series resonance, the circuit behaves like a:
A) Pure InductorB) Pure CapacitorC) Pure ResistorD) Open Circuit
Ans: C) Pure Resistor
At series resonance, inductive reactance (XL) and capacitive reactance (XC) are equal in magnitude but opposite in phase, cancelling each other out completely. Therefore, the circuit behaves purely as a resistor.
100. The resonant frequency (fr) of a series RLC circuit is:
A) 1 / (2π√LC)B) 2π√LCC) 1 / √LCD) √(L/C)
Ans: A) 1 / (2π√LC)
Equating XL = XC gives: 2πfL = 1 / (2πfC). Solving for frequency (f) yields the resonant frequency: fr = 1 / (2π√LC).
101. At series resonance, the impedance is:
A) MaximumB) MinimumC) ZeroD) Infinite
Ans: B) Minimum (Z = R)
Since Z = √[R^2 + (XL - XC)^2], when XL = XC at resonance, the reactive term becomes zero. The impedance reaches its minimum value, which is exactly equal to the resistance R.
102. At parallel resonance, the impedance of a practical tank circuit is:
A) MinimumB) MaximumC) ZeroD) Equal to R
Ans: B) Maximum
At parallel resonance, the reactive branch currents cancel out, resulting in a minimum current drawn from the main source. Since current is minimized at resonance, the equivalent parallel impedance reaches its Maximum value.
103. Q-factor of a series RLC circuit is:

The Quality (Q) factor measures the sharpness of the resonant peak. It is derived as: Q = ωr*L / R = (1 / √LC) * L / R = (1/R) * √(L/C).

The Q-factor and Bandwidth are inversely proportional. A higher Q-factor indicates a sharper resonant curve (narrower bandwidth), which translates to higher selectivity: BW = fr / Q.

The standard impedance formula is Z = R + j(XL - XC). If the imaginary part (X) is negative, it indicates that the capacitive reactance (XC) dominates, making the circuit capacitive.

Admittance can be expressed in complex form as Y = G + jB, where G is the real part (Conductance) and B is the imaginary part (Susceptance).

For a square wave, the maximum value, RMS value, and average value are all identical (Vrms = Vavg = Vm). Thus, the Form Factor = Vrms / Vavg = 1.0.

In a purely resistive circuit, the phase angle difference (φ) between voltage and current is zero. Thus, the power factor is cos φ = cos(0°) = 1 (Unity).

In a purely inductive circuit, the current lags the voltage by exactly 90° (φ = -90°). Thus, the power factor is cos(90°) = 0, which is termed Zero lagging.

At half-power frequencies (f1, f2), the current drops to 70.7% (Imax / √2) of its maximum value. Since power is proportional to the square of current ($P = I^2 R$), the power drops to exactly half of its maximum value.

4. Network Analysis

121. Mesh analysis is based on:
A) KCLB) KVLC) Ohm's LawD) Superposition
Ans: B) KVL
Mesh analysis is a technique used to find loop currents in a planar circuit. It is based directly on Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL), where voltage equations are written around closed loops.
122. Nodal analysis is based on:
A) KVLB) KCLC) Thevenin's LawD) Faraday's Law
Ans: B) KCL
Nodal analysis is used to find node voltages relative to a reference node. It is based directly on Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL), where current equations are written for each non-reference node.
123. The number of independent KVL equations for a network with 'b' branches and 'n' nodes is:
A) b - n + 1B) n - 1C) b - nD) n + 1
Ans: A) b - n + 1
According to network graph theory, the number of independent loops (and thus the number of independent KVL equations required) is given by: b - n + 1.
124. The number of independent KCL equations (Nodal equations) is:
A) b - n + 1B) n - 1C) b - 1D) n
Ans: B) n - 1
For a network with 'n' nodes, we designate one node as the reference (ground) node at 0V. This leaves exactly n - 1 independent nodes, requiring n - 1 nodal equations.
125. In graph theory, a 'tree' of a connected graph contains:
A) All nodes, no closed loopsB) All branches, no nodesC) Closed loopsD) Half the nodes
Ans: A) All nodes, no closed loops
By definition in graph theory, a tree is a connected subgraph that connects all nodes of the graph together without forming any closed loops.
126. Branches of a tree are called:
A) LinksB) ChordsC) TwigsD) Nodes
Ans: C) Twigs
The branches of a graph that are included in the tree are called Twigs. The remaining branches not in the tree are called links or chords.
127. Branches that are NOT part of a tree are called:
A) TwigsB) Links or ChordsC) RootsD) Leaves
Ans: B) Links or Chords
The branches that are excluded when forming a tree are called Links or Chords. Reconnecting a link to a tree creates a closed loop.
128. Addition of one link to a tree forms exactly one:
A) SupernodeB) Basic loop (Tie-set)C) Cut-setD) Node
Ans: B) Basic loop (Tie-set)
A tree contains no closed paths. When you add exactly one link to a tree, it forms a single closed path consisting of that link and several twigs. This is called a basic loop or a Tie-set.
129. Z-parameters are also known as:
A) Short-circuit parametersB) Open-circuit impedance parametersC) Transmission parametersD) Hybrid parameters
Ans: B) Open-circuit impedance parameters
Z-parameters (Impedances) are evaluated by opening the ports one by one (setting I1 = 0 or I2 = 0) to measure the resulting voltages. Hence, they are called open-circuit impedance parameters.
130. Y-parameters are also known as:
A) Open-circuit parametersB) Short-circuit admittance parametersC) Inverse hybridD) ABCD parameters
Ans: B) Short-circuit admittance parameters
Y-parameters (Admittances) are evaluated by short-circuiting the ports one by one (setting V1 = 0 or V2 = 0) to measure the resulting currents. Hence, they are called short-circuit admittance parameters.
131. In a two-port network, the condition for reciprocity in Z-parameters is:
A) Z11 = Z22B) Z12 = Z21C) Z11 * Z22 - Z12 * Z21 = 1D) Z12 = -Z21
Ans: B) Z12 = Z21
For any passive, bilateral two-port network, the transfer parameters from Port 1 to Port 2 and vice versa must be equal, which translates to Z12 = Z21.
132. The condition for a two-port network to be symmetrical in Z-parameters is:
A) Z11 = Z22B) Z12 = Z21C) Z11 = Z12D) Z22 = Z21
Ans: A) Z11 = Z22
A two-port network is symmetrical if its ports are electrically indistinguishable when viewed from the input or output terminals, which requires: Z11 = Z22.
133. ABCD parameters are widely used in:
A) Filter designB) Power transmission linesC) Transistor biasingD) Oscillator design
Ans: B) Power transmission lines
ABCD parameters (Transmission parameters) relate sending-end voltage and current directly to receiving-end voltage and current, making them ideal for modeling long power transmission lines.
134. For cascaded two-port networks, which parameters are multiplied?
A) Z-parametersB) Y-parametersC) ABCD parametersD) h-parameters
Ans: C) ABCD parameters
When two-port networks are connected in cascade, the overall transmission matrix is found simply by multiplying the individual ABCD matrices: [T_total] = [T_A] * [T_B].
135. For series connection of two-port networks, which parameters are added?
A) Z-parametersB) Y-parametersC) ABCD parametersD) h-parameters
Ans: A) Z-parameters
When two-port networks are connected in a series configuration, their inputs and outputs are in series, meaning their voltages add up while their currents remain identical. Thus, their individual Z-parameters are added.
136. For parallel connection of two-port networks, which parameters are added?
A) Z-parametersB) Y-parametersC) ABCD parametersD) g-parameters
Ans: B) Y-parameters
When two-port networks are connected in parallel, the voltages at the ports are identical, and the total terminal currents are the sum of individual currents. Thus, their individual admittance (Y) parameters are added.
137. The h-parameters (hybrid) are popular in analyzing:
A) Transmission linesB) TransformersC) BJT (Transistor) circuitsD) DC generators
Ans: C) BJT (Transistor) circuits
Hybrid parameters (h-parameters) are ideal for transistor modeling (like BJTs) because they combine an open-circuit input parameter and a short-circuit output parameter, which match physical transistor testing conditions.
138. The condition for reciprocity in ABCD parameters is:
A) A = DB) AD - BC = 1C) AD - BC = 0D) B = C
Ans: B) AD - BC = 1
For a network to be reciprocal under transmission (ABCD) parameters, the determinant of the transmission matrix must be equal to unity: AD - BC = 1.
139. The condition for symmetry in ABCD parameters is:
A) A = DB) AD - BC = 1C) A = BD) C = D
Ans: A) A = D
For a two-port network to be symmetrical, the input parameters must look identical from both ports. For ABCD parameters, this requires: A = D.
140. In Y-parameters, Y11 is defined as:

From the defining Y-parameter equation: I1 = Y11*V1 + Y12*V2. When we short-circuit the output port (setting V2 = 0), we isolate Y11 as the ratio of input current to input voltage: Y11 = I1 / V1.
141. Transient state in a circuit is caused by:
A) ResistorsB) Energy storing elements (L, C)C) Voltage sourcesD) Wires
Ans: B) Energy storing elements (L, C)
Resistors dissipate electrical energy immediately as heat. However, energy-storing elements like inductors (L) and capacitors (C) require finite time to charge or discharge, which creates transient states during switching.
142. An inductor does not allow sudden changes in:
A) VoltageB) CurrentC) ResistanceD) Power
Ans: B) Current
The voltage across an inductor is v = L * (di/dt). An instantaneous change in current (dt = 0) would require an infinite voltage, which is physically impossible. Therefore, inductors oppose sudden changes in current.
143. A capacitor does not allow sudden changes in:
A) CurrentB) PowerC) VoltageD) Charge polarity instantly
Ans: C) Voltage
The current through a capacitor is i = C * (dv/dt). An instantaneous change in voltage (dt = 0) would require an infinite current spike. Therefore, capacitors oppose sudden changes in voltage.

In a series RC transient response, the exponential decay is governed by e^(-t/RC), where the time constant τ = R * C is the time required for the capacitor to charge up to 63.2% of its steady-state value.

Ans: B) L/R
The series RL transient growth/decay is governed by e^(-t / (L/R)), where the time constant is defined as the ratio of inductance to resistance: τ = L/R.

In DC steady state (t = ∞), the capacitor is fully charged to the source voltage, and current flow stops completely. Therefore, the capacitor acts as an Open Circuit.

In DC steady state (t = ∞), the current becomes a constant DC value, meaning the rate of change of current di/dt is zero. Since inductor voltage is v = L*(di/dt), the voltage across it is zero, meaning it acts as a Short Circuit.

By evaluating the Laplace integral for a unit step: L{u(t)} = ∫ [0 to ∞] 1 * e^(-st) dt = [-1/s * e^(-st)] evaluated from 0 to ∞, which equals 1/s.
149. The Laplace transform of an impulse function δ(t) is:

The Dirac delta function (impulse) exists only at t=0 with infinite height and unit area. Evaluating its Laplace integral yields exactly 1.

5. Magnetically Coupled Circuits & Filters

161. Mutual inductance (M) exists between two coils when:
A) They are physically touchingB) Magnetic flux of one links the otherC) They are in seriesD) They have same resistance
Ans: B) Magnetic flux of one links the other
Mutual Inductance (M) is the phenomenon where a changing current in one coil induces a voltage in a nearby coil due to the magnetic flux of the first coil cutting across the turns of the second.
162. The coefficient of coupling (k) is related to L1, L2, and M by:

Ans: C) k = M / √(L1*L2)
The coefficient of coupling (k) measures the magnetic flux linkage efficiency. It is mathematically related to mutual and self-inductance by: M = k * √(L1 * L2), which translates to k = M / √(L1 * L2).
163. The maximum possible value of coupling coefficient (k) is:
A) 0B) 0.5C) 1D) Infinity
Ans: C) 1 (Perfect coupling)
The coupling coefficient (k) ranges from 0 to 1. A value of k = 1 represents perfect coupling, meaning 100% of the magnetic flux produced by one coil links the other without any magnetic leakage.
164. Two coils in series aiding have equivalent inductance Leq =
A) L1 + L2 + MB) L1 + L2 + 2MC) L1 + L2 - 2MD) L1 + L2
Ans: B) L1 + L2 + 2M
In a series aiding connection, currents flow through both coils such that their individual magnetic fluxes support each other. This results in an equivalent inductance of: Leq = L1 + L2 + 2M.
165. Two coils in series opposing have equivalent inductance Leq =
A) L1 + L2 - 2MB) L1 + L2 + 2MC) L1 - L2 - MD) L1 + L2
Ans: A) L1 + L2 - 2M
In a series opposing connection, the current flows such that the magnetic fields of the two coils oppose each other. This reduces the overall inductance, giving: Leq = L1 + L2 - 2M.
166. Dot convention is used to determine:
A) Current magnitudeB) Voltage magnitudeC) Polarity of mutually induced voltageD) Coil resistance
Ans: C) Polarity of mutually induced voltage
The dot convention is a standard marking system used to easily determine the relative polarities of mutually induced voltages in coupled coils without needing to know the physical winding directions.
167. A filter is a circuit designed to:
A) Amplify signalsB) Rectify AC to DCC) Pass specific frequencies and block othersD) Generate oscillations
Ans: C) Pass specific frequencies and block others
An electrical filter is a frequency-selective network designed to allow signals within a specified frequency band (passband) to go through while attenuating or blocking all other frequencies (stopband).
168. A Low Pass Filter (LPF) allows frequencies:
A) Above cut-off frequencyB) Below cut-off frequencyC) Between two frequenciesD) Only at resonance
Ans: B) Below cut-off frequency
A Low Pass Filter (LPF) permits low frequencies (including DC, 0 Hz) below its specific cut-off frequency (fc) to pass with minimal loss while heavily attenuating frequencies above fc.
169. A High Pass Filter (HPF) allows frequencies:
A) Below cut-off frequency (fc)B) Above cut-off frequency (fc)C) DC onlyD) A specific narrow band
Ans: B) Above cut-off frequency (fc)
A High Pass Filter (HPF) blocks low-frequency components (including DC) below its cut-off frequency (fc) and allows high-frequency signals above fc to pass through.
170. A Band Pass Filter (BPF) passes frequencies:
A) Below lower cut-offB) Above upper cut-offC) Between lower and upper cut-offD) Outside a certain band
Ans: C) Between lower and upper cut-off
A Band Pass Filter (BPF) allows a specific band of frequencies situated between a lower cut-off frequency (fc1) and an upper cut-off frequency (fc2) to pass, while blocking all outer frequencies.
171. A Band Stop Filter (BSF) is also known as:
A) Band Reject FilterB) Notch FilterC) Both A and BD) All pass filter
Ans: C) Both A and B
A Band Stop Filter blocks a specific range of frequencies. It is commonly referred to as a Band Reject Filter, or a Notch Filter if the bandwidth being blocked is extremely narrow.
172. In an ideal filter, the attenuation in the passband is:

Ans: B) Zero
An ideal filter should pass desired signals with absolutely no attenuation or loss. Therefore, the passband attenuation (α) must be exactly Zero (0 dB).
173. The frequency at which the output power falls to half of its max value is called:

Ans: B) Cut-off frequency (-3dB frequency)
The cut-off frequency (fc) represents the transition boundary where the output power drops to exactly half of its maximum value, which corresponds to a 3 dB reduction in output voltage (70.7% amplitude).
174. A constant-k low pass filter is composed of:
A) Series L, Shunt CB) Series C, Shunt LC) Series R, Shunt CD) Series L, Shunt R
Ans: A) Series L, Shunt C
At low frequencies, an inductor has low reactance (short) and a capacitor has high reactance (open), allowing signals to pass. Thus, a series Inductor (L) and shunt Capacitor (C) combination forms a Low Pass Filter.
175. A constant-k high pass filter is composed of:
A) Series L, Shunt CB) Series C, Shunt LC) Series L, Shunt LD) Series C, Shunt C
Ans: B) Series C, Shunt L
A capacitor blocks DC/low frequencies (acting as an open) and passes high frequencies, while a shunt inductor shunts low frequencies to ground. Thus, a series Capacitor (C) and shunt Inductor (L) form a High Pass Filter.
176. The property Z1*Z2 = R0^2 applies to which filter?
A) m-derived filterB) Constant-k filterC) Active filterD) Butterworth filter
Ans: B) Constant-k filter (where R0 is design impedance)
Constant-k filters are designed based on the condition that the product of the series arm impedance (Z1) and shunt arm impedance (Z2) is a constant (k^2 = R0^2) that is completely independent of frequency.
177. m-derived filters are designed to solve which problem of constant-k filters?
A) Slow attenuation rate at cut-offB) High costC) Size issuesD) Low power handling
Ans: A) Slow attenuation rate at cut-off
Constant-k filters have a very slow transition from the passband to the stopband. The m-derived filter solves this by incorporating a parameter 'm' to create a much sharper cut-off transition.
178. Active filters use operational amplifiers because they provide:
A) InductanceB) Gain and avoid loading effectsC) Pure resistanceD) Higher voltage
Ans: B) Gain and avoid loading effects
Op-Amps in active filters provide high input impedance (preventing loading on preceding stages), low output impedance, and adjustable signal gain. This eliminates the need for bulky inductors.
179. Passive filters are made up of:
A) R, L, CB) Op-amps, R, CC) TransistorsD) Diodes
Ans: A) R, L, C
Passive filters are constructed entirely using passive components: Resistors (R), Inductors (L), and Capacitors (C). They require no external DC power supply to operate.
180. The decibel (dB) is a unit of:
A) VoltageB) CurrentC) Power ratio or AttenuationD) Frequency
Ans: C) Power ratio or Attenuation
The decibel (dB) is a logarithmic unit used to express the ratio of two levels of power, voltage, or current. It is commonly used to quantify attenuation, gain, and loss in filter networks.
181. For an ideal transformer, the coupling coefficient k is:
A) 0B) 0.5C) 1.0D) 1.5
Ans: C) 1.0
An ideal transformer assumes zero magnetic flux leakage, meaning all of the flux generated by the primary winding perfectly links the secondary winding. Thus, k is exactly 1.0.
182. A transformer transfers electrical energy from one circuit to another without changing:
A) VoltageB) CurrentC) FrequencyD) Impedance
Ans: C) Frequency
While a transformer steps voltage and current levels up or down through electromagnetic induction, it operates on a constant magnetic flux cycle, meaning the AC frequency remains unchanged.
183. Mutual inductance M is measured in:
A) FaradsB) OhmsC) HenrysD) Webers
Ans: C) Henrys
Just like self-inductance (L), mutual inductance (M) represents opposition to changing currents via magnetic induction. Its standard SI unit is the Henry (H).
184. If the physical distance between two coupled coils is increased, M will:
A) IncreaseB) DecreaseC) Remain constantD) Become infinite
Ans: B) Decrease
Increasing the physical distance between coils reduces the amount of shared magnetic flux. This increases magnetic leakage and lowers the coupling coefficient (k), thereby decreasing the mutual inductance (M).
185. A cut-off frequency is also called the:

In network analysis and frequency response plotting (like Bode plots), the cut-off frequency is known by several interchangeable terms: break frequency, corner frequency, and half-power frequency.
186. In a T-section filter, the series arms are:

A symmetrical T-section filter is split into two equal series arms. The total series impedance Z1 is divided equally between these two branches, making each series arm Z1/2.
187. In a π-section filter, the shunt arms are:
A) Z2B) Z2/2C) 2*Z2D) Z1

In a symmetrical π-section filter, the shunt impedance (Z2) is split into two parallel branches. For the parallel combination of the two ends to match the network, each shunt branch must have an impedance of 2*Z2.
188. Attenuation (α) in the passband of an ideal filter is:

Ans: C) Zero
The passband is the range of frequencies that should pass through the filter completely unaltered. Thus, an ideal filter has zero attenuation (α = 0) in its passband.
189. Phase shift (β) in the stopband of a constant-k filter is:
A) ZeroB) π or -π radiansC) π/2 radiansD) Infinity
Ans: B) π or -π radians
In the stopband of a constant-k filter, the transmission phase shift remains constant at exactly 180° out of phase, which is represented as π or -π radians.
190. Characteristic impedance (Z0) of a symmetrical network is:
A) √(Z_oc * Z_sc)B) Z_oc / Z_scC) Z_sc / Z_ocD) Z_oc + Z_sc
Ans: A) √(Z_oc * Z_sc)
The characteristic impedance (Z0) of a symmetrical network is defined as the geometric mean of its open-circuit impedance (Z_oc) and short-circuit impedance (Z_sc): Z0 = √(Z_oc * Z_sc).
191. The primary purpose of a terminating half-section (m-derived) is to:
A) Increase gainB) Provide constant characteristic impedanceC) Increase bandwidthD) Decrease cost
Ans: B) Provide constant characteristic impedance
In filter cascading, terminating half-sections (m-derived, typically m=0.6) are placed at the inputs and outputs to provide a nearly flat characteristic impedance across the passband, ensuring excellent impedance matching and minimal reflection losses.
192. Magnetic coupling is utilized effectively in:
A) CapacitorsB) TransformersC) ResistorsD) Diodes
Ans: B) Transformers
Transformers are designed to transfer electrical energy from one winding to another entirely through the medium of mutual magnetic flux coupling.
193. Two coils have L1=4H, L2=9H and k=0.5. What is M?

Ans: A) 3H
Using the mutual inductance coupling formula: M = k * √(L1 * L2) = 0.5 * √(4 * 9) = 0.5 * √36 = 0.5 * 6 = 3H.

According to the dot convention, if the currents enter the dotted terminals of both coils, the mutually induced magnetic fluxes will align in the same direction, aiding the self-induced voltages.
195. Filters are widely used in:

196. An m-derived filter has an m value strictly between:

Ans: A) 0 and 1
To satisfy the physical realizability of the filter components (ensuring positive values for L and C), the design parameter 'm' must be strictly bounded between the limits of 0 and 1.

A Band Pass Filter can be formed by cascading a High Pass Filter (to block low frequencies) with a Low Pass Filter (to block high frequencies) such that their passband regions overlap.
